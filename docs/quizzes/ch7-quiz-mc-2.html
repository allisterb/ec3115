EC3115: Money, Inflation and Welfare (Set 2)

51. In the classical model, the assumption that money is neutral implies that the aggregate supply curve is:

Monetary neutrality means that changes in the money supply do not affect real variables, including real output. This is a core feature of the classical model, where fully flexible wages and prices ensure that output is always at its full-employment or "natural" level, \(y^*\). Therefore, the aggregate supply curve is a vertical line at \(y^*\), indicating that output is independent of the price level. Source: McCallum, p. 93.

52. The total real revenue from the inflation tax can be decomposed into which two components?

As shown by Cagan, the total real revenue from inflation is the sum of two parts. The first is the purchasing power of the new money being printed (\(dM/dt \times 1/P\)). The second is the capital loss imposed on the holders of the existing money stock as inflation erodes its value (\(-d(M/P)/dt\)). Source: Cagan, p. 78.

53. In the Cagan model, the parameter \(\beta\) in the adaptive expectations formula \(dE/dt = \beta(C-E)\) represents:

The parameter \(\beta\) is the "coefficient of expectation." It determines how quickly people revise their expectations of inflation (E) based on the error between the actual inflation rate (C) and their previous expectation. A higher \(\beta\) means expectations adjust more rapidly to recent experience. Source: Cagan, p. 37.

54. Why might real money balances RISE towards the very end of a hyperinflation?

This is a puzzle that the simple Cagan model cannot explain. The most plausible hypothesis is that as rumors of a credible currency reform spread, the public anticipates that the high inflation will soon end. The expected cost of holding money falls, leading to an increase in the demand for real balances even while the current inflation rate is still extremely high. Source: Cagan, p. 55.

55. The concept of "monetarist superneutrality" as an empirical idea suggests that:

Monetarist superneutrality is the idea that an inflationary equilibrium is largely invariant to the rate of monetary growth. Once transition effects are over, variations in money growth are reflected predominantly in inflation, not in real variables like output growth. The cross-country study by McCandless and Weber, cited by Mizen, supports this by finding no consistent relationship between long-run inflation and real output growth. Source: Mizen, p. 171.

56. In Friedman's analysis of the demand for money, what is the primary budget constraint for an ultimate wealth-holder?

Friedman treated money as a capital asset, one of several ways to hold wealth. Therefore, the demand for money is a portfolio decision. The relevant budget constraint for a portfolio choice is the total wealth that must be allocated among different assets, not the flow of current income. Source: Mizen, p. 154.

57. What is a key distinction between seigniorage and the inflation tax in a growing economy?

Seigniorage is the government's revenue from its monopoly on money creation. In a growing economy with zero inflation, the government can still earn seigniorage by printing money to meet the growing demand for real balances. The inflation tax, however, is specifically the revenue gained from the erosion of real balances due to positive inflation. Thus, seigniorage can be positive even if the inflation tax is zero. General concept from course materials.

58. According to the Dornbusch model, a key factor that can turn high inflation into an explosive hyperinflation is:

Dornbusch emphasizes the role of institutional adaptation. As inflation rises, wage contracts, financial contracts, and tax collection lags all shorten. This institutional disintegration makes the inflation rate far more sensitive and volatile. A small shock can trigger a shift to even shorter contracts, causing the inflation process to become explosive. Source: Dornbusch, p. 22.

59. In the welfare cost diagram for inflation, the area representing the inflation tax revenue is a rectangle with height equal to the inflation rate and width equal to:

The inflation tax revenue is the tax rate (the rate of inflation, \(\pi\)) multiplied by the tax base. The tax base is the amount of real money people continue to hold during the inflation. Therefore, the revenue is \(\pi \times (M/P)_1\), where \((M/P)_1\) is the new, lower level of real balances. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 92.

60. The classical dichotomy refers to the idea that:

The classical dichotomy is the theoretical separation of the economy into a real side and a nominal side. In this view, real variables like output, employment, and the real interest rate are determined purely by real factors (technology, preferences, resource endowments). The monetary sector, including the money supply, only determines nominal variables like the price level and the nominal wage rate. Source: McCallum, p. 100 (footnote 15).

61. In the Cagan model, if the coefficient of expectation \(\beta\) is very high (close to 10.0), it implies that:

The coefficient \(\beta\) measures the speed of adjustment. A very high \(\beta\) means that people adjust their expectations very quickly to the most recent inflation experience. In the limit, as \(\beta\) approaches infinity, the expected rate of change in prices becomes equal to the current actual rate of change, and the lag in expectations disappears. Source: Cagan, p. 41.

62. Which of the following is NOT one of the three main sources of runaway inflation discussed by Mizen?

Mizen (p. 172) lists three potential sources for runaway inflation: currency depreciation, wage-push pressures, and budget deficits with money creation. While an oil price shock is a significant supply-side shock that can cause inflation, it is not listed as one of the primary, interacting hypotheses for the German hyperinflation experience discussed in the text. Source: Mizen, p. 172.

63. In the IS-LM model, if the demand for money is perfectly interest-inelastic (a vertical LM curve), what is the effect of an increase in the money supply?

With a vertical LM curve, money demand \(M/P = L(y)\) does not depend on the interest rate. An increase in the nominal money supply M shifts the LM curve to the right. To restore equilibrium at the same level of output y, the price level P must rise proportionally to bring the real money supply M/P back to its original level. This is a key tenet of the quantity theory of money. Source: McCallum, p. 84-85.

64. The 'real balance effect' breaks the superneutrality of money because:

The real balance effect introduces real money balances (M/P) as an argument in the consumption function. A higher rate of inflation reduces M/P. This reduction in real wealth causes households to consume less and save more, shifting the IS curve. This shift leads to a change in the equilibrium real interest rate, thus breaking superneutrality. Source: McCallum, p. 121.

65. According to Friedman's empirical propositions, how long is the total average delay between a change in monetary growth and a subsequent change in the rate of inflation?

Friedman famously described the lags in monetary policy as "long and variable." He proposed that a change in monetary growth first affects output after about 6-9 months. The effect on prices comes later, after another 9-15 months. The total delay for inflation to respond is therefore the sum of these, averaging 15 to 24 months. Source: Mizen, p. 160 (Table 7.1).

66. In the context of the inflation tax, the "tax base" is:

The inflation tax is a tax on holding money. The base of any tax is the thing being taxed. In this case, the government extracts resources based on the stock of real money (M/P) that the public holds. The tax rate is the rate of inflation. Source: Mizen, p. 177; Cagan, p. 78.

67. The argument that inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" means that:

This famous quote by Milton Friedman means that a sustained rise in the price level (inflation) can only occur if the quantity of money grows faster than the quantity of real output. While other factors (like fiscal policy or supply shocks) can cause temporary price changes, they can only cause sustained inflation if they lead to a sustained increase in the rate of monetary growth. Source: Mizen, p. 161 (Table 7.1, proposition 8).

68. In the Cagan model, the elasticity of demand for real balances with respect to the expected rate of inflation is given by \(-aE\). This implies that the demand for money becomes more elastic as:

The elasticity is \(-aE\). Since elasticity is considered in absolute value, it is \(|-aE| = aE\). As the expected rate of inflation, E, increases, the value of \(aE\) also increases (since \(a > 0\)). This means that at higher rates of expected inflation, the public's demand for real balances becomes more responsive (more elastic) to further changes in expected inflation. Source: Cagan, p. 35.

69. According to the subject guide, which author is primarily associated with the model of very rapid, but non-explosive, continuing inflation?

The subject guide explains that while Cagan's model is used to demonstrate how hyperinflations can become explosive, a simpler model by Dornbusch can explain the phenomenon of very rapid but stable, continuing inflation. This model demonstrates a stable equilibrium based on a government deficit financed by money creation. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 94.

70. In the Dornbusch model of chronic inflation, where \(\pi = gV\), what happens if the government deficit (g) increases?

In the simple model \(\pi = gV\), the inflation rate is directly proportional to the size of the government deficit (g) that is financed by money creation. A larger deficit requires a higher inflation rate to generate the necessary seigniorage revenue, assuming velocity (V) is constant or also rises with inflation. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 95.

71. The analysis of the welfare cost of inflation using a consumer surplus diagram assumes that the social cost of creating additional fiat money is:

The welfare cost arises because there is a divergence between the private cost and social cost of holding money. The private cost is the nominal interest rate foregone. The social cost of creating additional units of fiat money (paper and ink) is negligible, assumed to be approximately zero. This divergence means that individuals economize on money holdings even though it is socially costless to provide more. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 92.

72. In Friedman's description of the transmission mechanism, the "income effect" on interest rates refers to:

Following the initial "liquidity effect" which lowers interest rates, the monetary expansion stimulates spending and nominal income. This increased economic activity raises the demand for loans and credit, which in turn puts upward pressure on interest rates, causing them to rise back towards their original level. Friedman called this the "income effect". Source: Mizen, p. 170.

73. The existence of the Mundell-Tobin effect implies that the IS curve, when drawn in (r, Y) space where r is the real interest rate, will:

The Mundell-Tobin effect works through a wealth effect in the consumption function. A decrease in real money balances (M/P) is a decrease in real wealth. This causes households to save more and consume less at any given real interest rate. The increase in saving shifts the IS curve to the left (or inwards). Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 90.

74. In his 1956 restatement, Friedman argued that the velocity of money is:

A central tenet of Friedman's restatement is that the demand for money (and thus velocity, its reciprocal) is a stable and predictable function of a limited number of variables, such as wealth, the returns on alternative assets, and expected inflation. It is not a constant number, but its movements are systematic and predictable. Source: Mizen, p. 159.

75. The primary reason hyperinflations are ultimately stopped is that:

Hyperinflations cause immense economic chaos and social upheaval. As Cagan and Dornbusch note, the disruption eventually becomes so severe that it creates the political will to undertake the drastic and painful fiscal and monetary reforms necessary for stabilization. The economic system verges on chaos, making a return to orthodox policy an economic and political necessity. Source: Cagan, p. 86; Dornbusch, p. 15.

76. If the demand for real money balances is highly inelastic with respect to the nominal interest rate, the welfare cost of a given rate of inflation will be:

The welfare cost is the area of the trapezoid under the demand curve. If demand is inelastic, it means that even as the interest rate (the cost of holding money) rises due to inflation, people do not significantly reduce their real money holdings. The reduction in quantity is small, so the area of the welfare loss trapezoid (C+E in the diagram) is also small. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 93.

77. In the classical model, if the labor supply is a positive function of the real wage, an increase in the capital stock (k) would lead to:

An increase in the capital stock raises the marginal product of labor (MPL). This shifts the labor demand curve (which is the MPL curve) to the right. With an upward-sloping labor supply curve, the new equilibrium will be at a higher real wage and a higher level of employment. The higher employment, combined with the more productive capital stock, leads to a higher level of output. Source: McCallum, p. 89-92.

78. The term "full liquidity" as used by Friedman refers to a situation where:

"Full liquidity" is the point of satiation in real money balances, where the marginal utility of holding an additional unit of money is zero. This occurs when the opportunity cost of holding money, the nominal interest rate, is also zero. At this point (L in the welfare diagram), the maximum possible social utility from money is achieved. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 92.

79. According to the monetarist view, how does fiscal policy (e.g., an increase in government spending financed by borrowing) primarily cause inflation?

In the monetarist framework, inflation is a monetary phenomenon. Fiscal policy, by itself, is not seen as a primary driver of inflation. An increase in government spending financed by borrowing simply reallocates spending from the private sector to the public sector. It only becomes inflationary if the government deficit is financed by money creation (i.e., the central bank prints money to buy the government debt). Source: Mizen, p. 161 (Table 7.1, proposition 9).

80. In the Cagan model, the relationship \(d \log(M/P)/dt = -\alpha \cdot dE/dt\) implies that:

This equation is the time derivative of the basic Cagan money demand function. It states that the percentage change in real money balances (the rate at which people are flying from or to money) is proportional to the change in the expected rate of inflation. If people expect inflation to accelerate (dE/dt > 0), they will reduce their real balances (d log(M/P)/dt < 0). Source: General derivation from Cagan model.

81. If a country experiences a one-time jump in its price level with no change in the long-run inflation rate, this is most likely due to:

In the quantity theory framework (MV=PY), a one-time, permanent increase in the level of the money supply (M), with V and Y constant in the long run, must be met with a one-time, proportional increase in the price level (P) to restore equilibrium. It does not, by itself, change the ongoing rate of inflation. Source: McCallum, p. 95.

82. The efficiency of the inflation tax is low (i.e., the welfare cost per unit of revenue is high) when:

If money demand is highly elastic, a small increase in the inflation rate (the tax rate) will cause a very large reduction in real money holdings (the tax base). This leads to a large welfare cost (large area C+E) for a small amount of revenue (area B-E). Therefore, the tax is highly inefficient. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 93.

83. In the classical model, the demand for labor is determined by the point where:

A profit-maximizing firm in a competitive market will hire labor up to the point where the additional output produced by the last worker (the marginal product of labor, MPL or f'(n)) is equal to the cost of hiring that worker in real terms (the real wage, W/P). This condition is expressed as f'(n) = W/P. Source: McCallum, p. 91.

84. Which historical event is often cited as a key reason for the decline of simple Keynesianism and the revival of the quantity theory in the 1970s?

The 1970s experienced stagflation, a combination of high inflation and high unemployment. This contradicted the simple Phillips Curve trade-off that was a cornerstone of many Keynesian policy models at the time. Monetarist theories, which emphasized monetary growth as the cause of inflation and the concept of a natural rate of unemployment, appeared to offer a better explanation for these events, leading to a revival of the quantity theory. Source: Mizen, p. 151 (implied); Palgrave, p. 258.

85. In Friedman's view, the demand for money by a business enterprise is best viewed as a decision related to:

Friedman distinguishes between money demand by ultimate wealth-holders (a portfolio choice) and by firms. For firms, money is a capital asset that provides productive services (like facilitating transactions), similar to machinery or inventories. Therefore, the decision of how much money to hold is a capital budgeting problem, analyzed as part of the theory of production. Source: Mizen, p. 154; Palgrave, p. 253.

86. The primary channel through which an external debt crisis can lead to high domestic inflation is:

When a government can no longer borrow from abroad to service its existing external debt, it must acquire the necessary foreign exchange. If it cannot raise taxes or cut spending sufficiently, its only recourse is to print domestic money to buy the foreign exchange. This monetization of the external debt service acts just like the monetization of a fiscal deficit, leading to inflationary pressures. Source: Dornbusch, p. 21.

87. If a country experiences a one-time jump in its price level with no change in the long-run inflation rate, this is most likely due to:

In the quantity theory framework (MV=PY), a one-time, permanent increase in the level of the money supply (M), with V and Y constant in the long run, must be met with a one-time, proportional increase in the price level (P) to restore equilibrium. It does not, by itself, change the ongoing rate of inflation. Source: McCallum, p. 95.

88. In the context of hyperinflation, the term "dollarization" refers to:

Dollarization (or currency substitution) is a form of financial adaptation to high inflation. As the domestic currency rapidly loses its value, the public spontaneously starts using a more stable foreign currency, often the U.S. dollar, for contracting, pricing, and transactions. This erodes the domestic inflation tax base. Source: Dornbusch, p. 22.

89. The analysis of the return on equities in Friedman's framework treats them as equivalent to:

Friedman models an equity as a claim to a real income stream, in contrast to a bond which is a claim to a nominal stream. He therefore treats it as being like a bond that is indexed to the price level, meaning its nominal payments and principal value rise with inflation to protect its real value. Source: Mizen, p. 156.

90. A key finding from Cagan's empirical study of seven hyperinflations was that:

Despite the chaotic conditions, Cagan found that his relatively simple model, where real money demand depends on adaptively formed expectations of inflation, explained the data for most months with a high degree of accuracy (high R-squared values). This was strong evidence that even under extreme stress, money demand behavior remained systematic and was not irrational. Source: Cagan, p. 43 (Table 3).

91. In the welfare analysis of inflation, moving from a positive inflation rate to the optimal rate (Friedman's rule) involves:

Moving to the optimal quantity of money eliminates the incentive for people to inefficiently economize on real balances. The resources previously wasted on cash management are freed for productive use or leisure. This represents the elimination of the deadweight loss (the welfare cost triangle/trapezoid), which is a net gain for society. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 92.

92. The Cambridge cash-balance version of the quantity equation (M = kPY) emphasizes the role of money as a:

The Cambridge approach, in contrast to Fisher's transactions approach, focuses on why people choose to *hold* money. It emphasizes money's function as a store of value or a "temporary abode of purchasing power" in the interval between receiving income and making expenditures. The parameter 'k' represents the fraction of income people desire to hold in this form. Source: Palgrave, p. 250.

93. A key reason that even mild, steady inflation is considered undesirable in actual economies is that:

McCallum notes that the small calculated welfare costs of steady inflation seem to conflict with the public's strong dislike of it. A major reason is that real-world inflation is rarely steady and anticipated. Furthermore, it interacts with tax systems based on nominal magnitudes (e.g., taxes on nominal capital gains), which distorts incentives and misallocates resources. It also encourages people to waste valuable resources trying to forecast inflation. Source: McCallum, p. 130.

94. In the context of the classical model, what does the term "dichotomize" mean?

In the simple classical model presented by McCallum, the system "dichotomizes" or splits into two. The real variables (output, employment, real wage) are determined entirely on the supply side in the labor market. The monetary side (IS-LM or quantity equation) then determines the nominal variables (price level, nominal interest rate) without any feedback to the real side. Source: McCallum, p. 100.

95. According to Dornbusch, why is an incomes policy (like freezing wages and the exchange rate) often a necessary component of a stabilization program?

Dornbusch argues that in high-inflation economies, inflation has significant inertia due to overlapping wage contracts and formal or informal indexation. A purely fiscal and monetary contraction would cause a severe recession before this inertia is broken. An incomes policy acts as a coordinating device to break the inflationary momentum quickly, supplementing the necessary budget cuts and giving stabilization a chance to start. Source: Dornbusch, p. 25, 27.

96. If the government finances its deficit entirely by printing money, the real value of the deficit is equal to:

If the deficit (gY) is financed entirely by money creation (\(\dot{M}/P\)), then the real deficit is equal to the real value of the new money being issued. This new money is the source of the government's command over real resources. Source: Dornbusch, p. 18 (equation 1).

97. In the context of the classical model, an increase in government purchases (g) financed by taxes will:

In the classical model, output (y) is fixed at the full-employment level by the labor market. An increase in government spending (g) shifts the IS curve to the right. To restore goods market equilibrium at the fixed level of output, the real interest rate (r) must rise to "crowd out" an equivalent amount of private investment and consumption. Source: McCallum, p. 86, 95.

98. The primary reason that the simple Keynesian model with a fixed nominal wage is unsuitable for analyzing steady, ongoing inflation is that:

The core of the simple Keynesian model is the assumption of a sticky or predetermined nominal wage (W). In an environment of steady inflation, where the price level (P) is constantly rising, the real wage (W/P) would be constantly falling. It is implausible that workers would accept a fixed nominal wage under such conditions. A sensible analysis requires modifying the model to allow for ongoing nominal wage increases. Source: McCallum, p. 113.

99. In the Cagan model, if the money supply is held constant but people suddenly expect a currency reform that will stop inflation, what will happen to the current price level?

The expectation of a future currency reform means the expected rate of inflation (E) falls. According to the money demand function \(M/P = e^{-\alpha E}\), a fall in E leads to an increase in the demand for real money balances (M/P). Since the nominal money supply (M) is held constant, the only way for real balances (M/P) to increase is for the current price level (P) to fall. Source: Cagan, p. 55.

100. In the Dornbusch model of chronic inflation where \(\pi = gV\), if velocity (V) is an increasing function of inflation (\(V(\pi)\)), what ensures a stable low-inflation equilibrium?

For the low-inflation equilibrium (Point E in the diagram) to be stable, a small increase in inflation must set in motion forces that return it to equilibrium. If inflation rises slightly, velocity V rises. This causes inflation to rise further (since \(\pi = gV\)). For stability, this feedback must be dampened. This occurs if the rise in V is less than the rise in \(\pi/g\). Graphically, this means the \(V(\pi)\) curve must intersect the \(\pi = gV\) line from above, i.e., it must be flatter than the line at the intersection point. Source: EC3115 - Ch 7 Money, inflation and welfare-1.pdf, p. 95.
