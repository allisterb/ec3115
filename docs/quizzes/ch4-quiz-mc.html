EC3115 Monetary Economics

Quiz: The Supply of Money and Monetary Standards

1. Which of the following is a primary role of financial intermediaries?

Explanation: Financial intermediaries, like banks, exist to lower the costs associated with financial transactions. They achieve economies of scale in gathering information about borrowers and pooling the funds of many savers, making the process of lending and borrowing more efficient than direct lending.

2. On a commercial bank's balance sheet, where are customer deposits recorded?

Explanation: Deposits are liabilities for a bank because they represent money that the bank owes to its customers. The customers can demand this money back at any time (in the case of demand deposits).

3. Maturity transformation is a key function of banks. What does it involve?

Explanation: Maturity transformation is the practice of borrowing money on shorter timeframes than it is lent out. Banks accept deposits that are available on demand (short-term) and use these funds to make loans for longer periods (e.g., mortgages). This is crucial for financing long-term investment projects in the economy.

4. How does a central bank typically increase the monetary base?

Explanation: An open market purchase involves the central bank buying government bonds from commercial banks. The central bank pays for these bonds by crediting the commercial banks' reserve accounts, which directly increases the reserves (R) in the banking system and thus the monetary base (H = C + R).

5. If the central bank imposes a mandatory reserve ratio, how does this affect a commercial bank's ability to create money?

Explanation: A mandatory reserve ratio requires banks to hold a certain percentage of their deposits in reserve, which cannot be lent out. This limits the amount of new money that can be created from each new deposit, thereby constraining the overall money supply.

6. On a commercial bank's balance sheet, loans made to customers are considered:

Explanation: Loans are assets for a bank because they represent a claim on future income. The bank earns interest on these loans, making them a primary source of revenue.

7. Why must a bank's balance sheet always balance?

Explanation: The balance sheet is based on the fundamental accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Net Worth. This is an identity; it must hold true by definition. Assets represent the uses of funds, while liabilities and net worth represent the sources of funds.

8. The money multiplier is given by the formula \(m = \frac{1+c}{c+r}\). A decrease in the reserve-deposit ratio (r) will:

Explanation: The reserve-deposit ratio (r) is in the denominator of the money multiplier formula. A lower 'r' means that banks hold a smaller fraction of deposits in reserve and can lend out a larger portion. This leads to a larger expansion of deposits for each unit of monetary base, thus increasing the money multiplier.

9. What is 'high-powered money'?

Explanation: High-powered money, also known as the monetary base (H), consists of the liabilities of the central bank. It is the sum of currency held by the public (C) and the reserves of commercial banks (R). It is called 'high-powered' because a change in H can lead to a multiplied change in the total money supply (M).

10. Which of the following best describes the 'insurance' role of a financial intermediary?

Explanation: By making many different loans, a bank diversifies its risk. This allows it to offer depositors a stable, predictable rate of return, effectively insuring them against the risk of any single borrower defaulting. The risk is pooled and managed by the intermediary.

11. In the context of the money multiplier \(m = \frac{1+c}{c+r}\), an increase in the public's preference for holding currency over deposits (an increase in 'c') will:

Explanation: When the currency-deposit ratio (c) increases, it means more money is held by the public as cash rather than being deposited in banks. This reduces the amount of reserves in the banking system available for lending, weakening the re-lending cycle and thus decreasing the money multiplier.

12. A 'fiat money' system is one where the money:

Explanation: Fiat money has no intrinsic value and is not convertible into a commodity. Its value comes from social convention and the government's decree ('fiat') that it must be accepted as a means of payment.

13. What is the primary reason that maturity transformation is considered a risky activity for banks?

Explanation: Banks fund long-term, illiquid assets (loans) with short-term, liquid liabilities (deposits). If a large number of depositors demand their money back at once (a bank run), the bank may not be able to liquidate its long-term assets quickly enough to meet these demands, leading to insolvency.

14. If a central bank sells government bonds on the open market, what is the immediate effect on the banking system?

Explanation: An open market sale involves the central bank selling bonds to commercial banks. The banks pay for these bonds with their reserves, which are held at the central bank. This action directly reduces the quantity of reserves (R) in the banking system, contracting the monetary base.

15. Which of the following is an example of a bank's asset?

Explanation: Mortgage loans are assets for the bank because they represent a stream of future income from the borrower's repayments. Deposits are liabilities, and equity is the owners' stake.

16. Under a commodity money standard, like the gold standard, the value of money is ultimately determined by:

Explanation: In a commodity money system, the value of the monetary unit is tied to the value of a specific commodity. Therefore, factors affecting the supply of that commodity (like new discoveries) or the demand for it (for both monetary and non-monetary uses) determine its value and, by extension, the price level.

17. Why do banks exist, rather than savers lending directly to borrowers?

Explanation: Banks specialize in gathering information to assess the creditworthiness of borrowers (overcoming asymmetric information) and can pool the funds of many small savers to make large loans (achieving economies of scale), making the overall process far more efficient than direct lending.

18. If the required reserve ratio is 10% and a bank has $1,000 in deposits, what is the minimum amount of reserves it must hold?

Explanation: The required reserves are calculated as the required reserve ratio multiplied by the total deposits. In this case, 10% of $1,000 is $100.

19. The monetary base (H) is defined as:

Explanation: The monetary base (H), or high-powered money, consists of the liabilities of the central bank. It is composed of currency in the hands of the public (C) and the reserves of the banking system (R).

20. If banks decide to hold more excess reserves, what happens to the money multiplier?

Explanation: Holding more excess reserves means the reserve-deposit ratio (r) increases. Since 'r' is in the denominator of the money multiplier formula \(m = \frac{1+c}{c+r}\), an increase in 'r' will cause the value of the multiplier to decrease.

21. Which concept explains why a small increase in the monetary base can lead to a much larger increase in the money supply?

Explanation: The money multiplier effect describes the process of deposit creation in a fractional-reserve banking system. An initial increase in reserves allows a bank to lend, creating a new deposit at another bank, which in turn can lend a portion of that, and so on, leading to a multiplied expansion of the total money supply.

22. The 'credit spread' refers to:

Explanation: The credit spread is a primary source of a bank's profit. It is the margin between the interest earned on its assets (loans) and the interest paid on its liabilities (deposits).

23. In a competitive banking market with no reserve requirements or operating costs, what would you expect the credit spread to be?

Explanation: In a perfectly competitive market, banks would compete for depositors by raising deposit rates and for borrowers by lowering loan rates. This competition would drive the credit spread to zero, where profits are also zero (Loan Rate = Deposit Rate).

24. How does imposing a mandatory, non-interest-bearing reserve requirement affect the credit spread?

Explanation: Reserves are assets that earn no interest. To compensate for this loss of potential income, banks must charge a higher rate on their loans than they pay on deposits to remain profitable. This creates a positive credit spread, where \(i_L > i_D\).

25. Which of the following is NOT a component of the monetary base (H)?

Explanation: The monetary base (H) consists of central bank liabilities. Public deposits at commercial banks are liabilities of the commercial banks, not the central bank. They are a component of the broader money supply (M), but not the monetary base (H).

26. The process of financial intermediaries pooling funds from many small savers to make large loans is an example of:

Explanation: A single saver may not have enough funds to finance a large project like a factory. By collecting (pooling) deposits from thousands of savers, a bank achieves economies of scale and can make large-scale loans that would otherwise be impossible.

27. If the money multiplier is 2.5, a $10 billion increase in the monetary base will lead to what change in the money supply?

Explanation: The change in the money supply is the money multiplier multiplied by the change in the monetary base. \(\Delta M = m \times \Delta H\). So, \(2.5 \times \$10 \text{ billion} = \$25 \text{ billion}\).

28. Why is the service of 'maturity transformation' important for an economy?

Explanation: Many productive investments (like building infrastructure or factories) require long-term financing. However, most savers prefer to have ready access to their funds. Banks bridge this gap by transforming liquid, short-term deposits into illiquid, long-term loans, thereby facilitating crucial economic investment.

29. The total money supply (M) is defined as:

Explanation: The most common basic measure of the money supply (M1) is the sum of currency in the hands of the public (C) and checkable deposits held at financial institutions (D).

30. Under the gold standard, what was the role of the 'gold points'?

Explanation: The gold points were determined by the mint parity exchange rate plus or minus the costs of shipping gold between two countries. If the market exchange rate moved outside these points, it became profitable for arbitrageurs to ship gold, which would push the exchange rate back within the band.

31. Which entity's choices primarily determine the currency-deposit ratio (c)?

Explanation: The currency-deposit ratio (c = C/D) reflects the preferences of the public for holding their money as physical currency versus depositing it in the banking system. It is a behavioral variable dependent on public habits and confidence.

32. Which entity's choices primarily determine the reserve-deposit ratio (r)?

Explanation: The reserve-deposit ratio (r = R/D) is determined by two main factors: the mandatory reserve requirement set by the central bank, and the amount of excess reserves that commercial banks choose to hold for precautionary reasons.

33. If a bank has $500 in reserves and $2,000 in deposits, what is its reserve ratio?

Explanation: The reserve ratio is calculated as Total Reserves / Total Deposits. In this case, $500 / $2,000 = 0.25, or 25%.

34. An 'open market operation' is the purchase or sale of:

Explanation: Open market operations are the primary tool used by central banks to influence the monetary base. By buying or selling government bonds to/from the banking system, the central bank can inject or withdraw reserves.

35. If the currency-deposit ratio (c) is 0.2 and the reserve-deposit ratio (r) is 0.1, what is the value of the money multiplier (m)?

Explanation: Using the formula \(m = \frac{1+c}{c+r}\), we get \(m = \frac{1+0.2}{0.2+0.1} = \frac{1.2}{0.3} = 4.0\).

36. A bank's 'net worth' or 'equity' is calculated as:

Explanation: Net worth represents the owners' stake in the bank. It is the residual value remaining after subtracting liabilities (what the bank owes) from its assets (what the bank owns). Assets = Liabilities + Net Worth.

37. The introduction of a mandatory reserve ratio can be viewed as a 'tax' on bank lending because:

Explanation: By requiring banks to hold reserves that typically earn no interest, the central bank forces them to forgo the interest they could have earned by lending that money out. This implicit cost functions like a tax on the bank's operations.

38. If the public decides to withdraw cash from their bank accounts and hold more currency, what is the initial impact on the money supply (M) and the monetary base (H)?

Correct Answer: A

Explanation: This is a tricky point. Initially, this is just a change in the composition of both M and H, not their total size. For M (M=C+D), Currency (C) rises and Deposits (D) fall by the exact same amount, so M is unchanged. For H (H=C+R), Currency (C) rises and bank Reserves (R) fall by the exact same amount, so H is also unchanged. However, this action increases the currency-deposit ratio 'c', which will cause the money multiplier to fall, leading to a subsequent contraction of the money supply through the re-lending process.

39. The 'lender of last resort' is a function of:

Explanation: The central bank acts as the lender of last resort, providing liquidity to solvent but illiquid banks during a financial crisis to prevent bank failures and systemic collapse.

40. Which of the following would cause the money supply to expand?

Explanation: A decrease in the required reserve ratio lowers 'r', which increases the money multiplier. This means banks can lend out a larger fraction of deposits, leading to a greater expansion of the money supply for a given monetary base.

41. The 'unit of account' function of money refers to its role as:

Explanation: The unit of account function provides a common denominator for measuring the value of goods and services. It simplifies commerce by eliminating the need to calculate relative prices for every pair of goods, as in a barter system.

42. In a fractional-reserve banking system, the total amount of deposits will always be:

Explanation: Because banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend out the rest, the process of lending and re-depositing creates a total volume of deposits that is a multiple of the initial reserves in the system.

43. Why is a credit card not considered money in the same way as currency or a debit card transaction?

Explanation: A credit card transaction creates a loan from the issuing bank. The actual settlement of the debt (the final payment) happens later when you pay your credit card bill, typically using money from a deposit account. Therefore, the card itself is a means of deferring payment, not the final means of payment (money).

44. If the central bank wants to contract the money supply, it should:

Explanation: Selling government bonds (an open market sale) withdraws reserves from the banking system. This reduces the monetary base, which, via the money multiplier, leads to a larger contraction in the overall money supply.

45. The existence of financial intermediaries improves economic efficiency by:

Explanation: The core function of financial intermediaries is to facilitate the flow of funds from savers (who have excess funds) to borrowers (who have productive investment opportunities). This allocation of capital to its most productive uses is essential for economic growth.

46. If a bank's assets are $100 million and its net worth is $10 million, what are its liabilities?

Explanation: The balance sheet identity is Assets = Liabilities + Net Worth. Rearranging gives Liabilities = Assets - Net Worth. So, $100 million - $10 million = $90 million.

47. What is the key difference between a 'gold specie standard' and a 'gold bullion standard'?

Explanation: Under a gold specie standard, gold coins are in general circulation and banknotes are convertible into these coins. Under a gold bullion standard, gold coins do not circulate, and convertibility of currency into gold is typically restricted to large transactions for gold bars, often by specified groups.

48. If the money supply (M) is $2,000 billion and the monetary base (H) is $500 billion, what is the value of the money multiplier (m)?

Explanation: The relationship is M = m * H. Therefore, the money multiplier (m) can be found by dividing the money supply by the monetary base: m = M / H. So, $2,000 billion / $500 billion = 4.0.

49. A primary motivation for banks to engage in maturity transformation is:

Explanation: The yield curve is typically upward sloping, meaning interest rates on long-term instruments are higher than on short-term ones. By borrowing short (at low rates) and lending long (at high rates), banks can profit from the interest rate differential.

50. Which statement best describes the relationship between the monetary base and the money supply?

Explanation: Due to the money multiplier effect in a fractional-reserve banking system, the total money supply (M) is a multiple of the monetary base (H). The size of this multiple is determined by the money multiplier (m).
